A B S T R A C T 

Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) have recently emerged as a promising framework for integrating data-driven learning with physical knowledge. In this work, we propose a coupled PINN approach for the joint reconstruction of indoor temperature and humidity dynamics in greenhouse environments, together with simultaneous identification of key model parameters. The method incorporates a reduced-order physically motivated model into the learning process, enabling consistent estimation under sparse and noisy observations. The artificial intelligence contribution lies in the development of a coupled physics-informed neural learning framework that integrates governing dynamical constraints into neural network training, while the engineering application focuses on greenhouse climate state reconstruction and parameter identification. The proposed framework is evaluated on a controlled synthetic benchmark that mimics diurnal forcing conditions. Compared with a purely data-driven neural network baseline, the coupled PINN achieves improved reconstruction accuracy, reducing temperature and humidity errors while maintaining high coefficients of determination. The improvement is particularly pronounced in the humidity channel, where latent moisture dynamics are more difficult to infer from limited measurements. In addition to accurate state reconstruction, the method successfully recovers the domi-nant physical parameters governing the system dynamics, demonstrating its ability to learn interpretable representations beyond data interpolation. These results highlight the potential of physics-informed learning for greenhouse climate modeling and, more broadly, for data-scarce environmental systems. 

## 1. Introduction 

Greenhouse climate regulation is central to controlled-environment agriculture, where the ability to maintain suitable microclimatic conditions directly affects crop growth, resource efficiency, and operational reliability. Among the most important state variables are indoor temperature and relative humidity, since they influence transpiration, vapour pressure deficit, disease risk, condensation, and energy demand. For tomato cultivation, for instance, suitable ranges of temperature, humidity, and vapour pressure deficit have been extensively discussed in the greenhouse microclimate literature [10]. Accurate estimation and short-horizon reconstruction of these variables are therefore important for greenhouse monitoring, control, and digital-twin development. Classical greenhouse climate models are often derived from mechanistic energy and mass balance principles. Such models provide interpretability and are useful for control-oriented studies, but their calibration may require geometric, climatic, crop, actuator, and material parameters that are not always available in practice [1, 2, 12]. In parallel, data-driven approaches have become increasingly common for greenhouse climate prediction, including artificial neural networks, recurrent neural networks, LSTM/GRU models, and hybrid machine-learning models [11, 13, 14]. methods can be effective when large representative datasets are available, but their performance may deteriorate when sensor measurements are sparse, noisy, irregular, or collected under changing environmental conditions. Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) provide a natural compromise between mechanistic modeling and purely data-driven learning. By penalizing violations of differential-equation residuals during training, PINNs allow neural approximations to be constrained by available physical structure [4, 6, 9]. This idea is particularly relevant for greenhouse microclimate modeling, where reduced thermal and moisture balance relations are available, while complete physical information and dense sensing are often not. Although recent studies have begun to explore PINNs and hybrid physics-guided learning in agricultural and greenhouse applications [3, 8], coupled temperature–humidity state reconstruction and parameter identification under sparse noisy sensing remain less systematically studied. To the best of our knowledge, this is among the first works to develop a coupled physics-informed neural framework for the joint reconstruction of indoor temperature and relative humidity together with simultaneous dominant-parameter identification in reduced greenhouse climate dynamics under sparse and noisy observations. In this work, we propose a data-efficient PINN framework for the joint reconstruction of indoor temperature and relative humidity using a reduced coupled thermal–moisture balance model. The method is designed for low-data settings and is formulated to estimate both latent state trajectories and unknown physical parameters from sparse noisy measurements. A purely data-driven neural network with the same input–output architecture is used as a baseline, allowing a direct comparison of the effect of physics-based regularization. To enable rigorous quantitative assessment of both state reconstruction and parameter identification, we inten-tionally focus on a controlled synthetic benchmark generated from the reduced-order model. This choice is motivated by the inverse nature of the problem: in realistic settings, the true latent states and physical parameters are generally unavailable, which makes direct validation of reconstruction and identification accuracy difficult. By working in a synthetic but physically consistent setting, we obtain full access to ground-truth trajectories and coefficients, enabling a clean and reproducible benchmark evaluation. Accordingly, the present study should be interpreted as a methodological proof-of-concept and benchmark investigation, rather than as a fully field-calibrated deployment model. A central motivation for the present work is that purely data-driven neural models may achieve satisfactory interpolation performance when abundant clean observations are available, but they often lack physical consistency, interpretability, and robustness in data-sparse or noisy regimes. By contrast, PINNs incorporate the governing dynamics directly into the learning process, thereby regularizing the hypothesis space and promoting solutions that remain consistent with the underlying physical model. This is particularly important for coupled environmental systems, where hidden cross-variable interactions can strongly influence the observed dynamics and where interpretable parameter recovery may be as important as predictive accuracy itself. The main contributions of this work are summarized as follows: 

• We propose a coupled physics-informed neural learning framework for a reduced greenhouse climate model, in which temperature and humidity are learned jointly rather than independently. 

• The proposed formulation simultaneously performs latent-state reconstruction and parameter identification by embedding the governing coupled ordinary differential equations directly into the training objective. 

• We incorporate the unknown physical coefficients as trainable parameters within the PINN architecture, thereby preserving interpretability and enabling direct recovery of the reduced-order model parameters from partial and noisy observations. 

• We provide a systematic comparison against a purely data-driven neural-network baseline under controlled synthetic experiments, demonstrating the stabilizing and regularizing effect of physics-informed learning. 

• The study establishes a reproducible benchmark for physics-informed inverse modeling in coupled environ-mental dynamical systems and provides a foundation for future extensions to data assimilation and control in greenhouse environments. 

Related Work 

Greenhouse climate modeling has been widely studied in agricultural engineering and control. Early and classical approaches are based on energy and mass balance equations for variables such as air temperature, humidity, radiation, ventilation, and crop transpiration [1, 12]. These models are attractive because they provide physically interpretable parameters and can be incorporated into robust or model-predictive control strategies. However, detailed greenhouse

models often require quantities that are difficult to measure directly, including ventilation rates, crop transpiration parameters, covering material properties, and actuator-dependent exchange coefficients. Recent greenhouse research has increasingly emphasized sensing, automation, intelligent control, and data-driven prediction. Reviews on greenhouse environmental control and temperature prediction highlight the rapid growth of AI-based forecasting and control methods in controlled-environment agriculture [2, 14]. In particular, neural networks and recurrent architectures have been used to predict temperature and humidity from historical climate measurements and external forcing variables [7, 11, 13]. These methods often achieve strong prediction accuracy, but they typically rely on dense historical data and do not necessarily enforce physical consistency. Physics-informed learning addresses this limitation by embedding governing equations into the training loss. The original PINN formulation of Raissi et al. [9] introduced a general framework for solving forward and inverse differential-equation problems using neural networks constrained by physical residuals. Subsequent work has de-veloped PINNs and related scientific machine-learning methods for many engineering and physical systems [4, 6]. In greenhouse and agricultural systems, physics-guided learning is still comparatively less developed than in fluid mechanics or heat-transfer benchmarks, although recent studies have begun to apply PINNs to greenhouse ventilation and hybrid mechanistic–neural greenhouse prediction [3, 8]. Public greenhouse datasets have also made data-driven and physics-guided benchmarking more accessible. Exam-ples include greenhouse datasets containing indoor and outdoor temperature, humidity, actuator states, light intensity, and related climate variables [5]. Such datasets are valuable because they allow method development without new experimental data collection. Nevertheless, real greenhouse datasets are often incomplete: solar radiation, ventilation rate, crop transpiration, or actuator states may be missing or only available through proxies. This motivates reduced-order approaches that preserve dominant physical structure while remaining identifiable under partial observation. The present work contributes to this line of research by focusing on coupled temperature–humidity dynamics under sparse noisy sensing. Rather than proposing a high-fidelity greenhouse simulator, we develop a reduced physics-informed neural framework for joint state reconstruction and dominant-parameter estimation. This provides a middle ground between purely mechanistic modeling and unconstrained neural forecasting.

## 4. Synthetic Benchmark Study 

We first assess the proposed coupled PINN framework on a controlled synthetic benchmark, where the true indoor temperature and humidity trajectories, as well as the underlying model parameters, are known. This setting enables a direct evaluation of both (i) state reconstruction accuracy and (ii) parameter identification performance under sparse and noisy observations. 

4.1. Synthetic data generation 

The synthetic benchmark is generated from the reduced coupled greenhouse climate model introduced in Section 2. The simulation horizon is taken as 

𝑡 ∈ [0 , 72] hours ,

corresponding to a three-day observation window. The initial condition is set to 

𝑇 (0) = 22 , 𝐻 (0) = 70 .

The external forcings are prescribed as smooth periodic functions intended to mimic diurnal outdoor temperature and humidity fluctuations, together with a daytime solar-radiation proxy. Specifically, the outdoor temperature and outdoor relative humidity follow sinusoidal profiles, while the radiation proxy is active only during daylight hours. The resulting forcing trajectories used in the synthetic experiments are shown in Fig. 1. The proposed method is compared against a purely data-driven neural-network baseline (with the same input-output structure but without any physics-based residual constraints). Both models are trained using the same sparse noisy observations. For the coupled PINN, the total objective consists of the observation loss, the physics residual loss, and the initial-condition penalty, as described in Section 3. The primary goal of this synthetic experiment is twofold: 

• to determine whether the coupled PINN yields more accurate state reconstructions than a pure neural baseline under limited sensing, and

4.7. Discussion of the synthetic benchmark 

Taken together, the synthetic results indicate that the proposed coupled PINN offers a clear advantage over a purely data-driven neural model when observations are sparse and noisy. This advantage remains consistent under moderate perturbations of the observation noise, indicating that the learned representation is not overly sensitive to measurement uncertainty. The gains are modest but consistent in the temperature channel, and substantially larger in the humidity channel, which is typically more difficult to reconstruct from limited measurements. Compared with standard data-driven neural models and recurrent architectures commonly used in greenhouse climate prediction, the present formulation explicitly incorporates physical constraints into the learning process. While direct quantitative comparison with such models is beyond the scope of this controlled synthetic study, the observed improvements over a purely data-driven baseline are consistent with the expected regularization effect of physics-informed learning under sparse and noisy observations.

Moreover, the parameter-recovery experiment shows that the framework is not merely interpolating the data: it also learns a physically interpretable dynamical representation whose dominant exchange coefficients are recovered with high accuracy. While the weakest cross-coupling terms exhibit larger relative deviations—most notably 𝑏 4—their absolute magnitudes remain small, and the resulting trajectory reconstructions remain highly accurate. This is consistent with practical identifiability limitations in reduced-order models under sparse noisy sensing, and it does not diminish the overall effectiveness of the proposed method for state reconstruction and dominant-parameter identification. 

## 5. Discussion and Pathways to Real-Data Validation 

The present study focuses on a controlled synthetic benchmark in order to establish the core behavior of the proposed method in a setting where both the latent trajectories and the true physical parameters are known. This allows a rigorous evaluation of reconstruction accuracy and parameter identification performance, which would not be directly accessible in a purely observational study. At the same time, the intended application of the proposed framework is practical greenhouse microclimate monitoring under limited sensing. In real greenhouse datasets, not all exogenous variables are always available. For example, public datasets may omit direct measurements of solar radiation, actuator states, or explicit ventilation rates. In such cases, the reduced model introduced in Section 2 should be interpreted as an effective surrogate model rather than a full mechanistic simulator. This is a strength rather than a limitation: the model is deliberately designed to preserve the dominant thermal–moisture interactions while remaining identifiable under sparse or partially observed conditions. A natural next step is to validate the framework on public greenhouse datasets containing at least indoor temperature, indoor relative humidity, and a subset of outdoor or exogenous forcing variables. In such a setting, the same coupled PINN framework can be used with modest modifications: 

• missing forcings can be replaced by reduced effective proxies, 

• the parameter set can be simplified to reflect the available measurements, and 

• the evaluation can be performed on held-out time windows using reconstruction and prediction metrics. The synthetic results reported here strongly support this next step. In particular, the clear gains in the humidity channel suggest that the physics-based coupling may be especially beneficial in practical greenhouse applications, where humidity is often more difficult to reconstruct robustly than temperature under sparse sensing. For submission to a highly application-oriented journal, it would be natural to complement the present synthetic benchmark with at least one real-data case study. However, even in its current form, the present work establishes a clear methodological contribution: a data-efficient, physically interpretable PINN framework for coupled greenhouse climate reconstruction and dominant-parameter identification. 

## 6. Conclusion 

This work introduced a coupled physics-informed neural network framework for the reconstruction of greenhouse climate dynamics under sparse and noisy observations. By embedding a reduced-order physical model into the learning process, the proposed approach enables simultaneous estimation of latent states and system parameters within a unified formulation. The synthetic benchmark study demonstrates that the coupled PINN consistently outperforms a purely data-driven neural network baseline. The gains are moderate but systematic for temperature reconstruction and substantially larger for humidity, indicating that the incorporation of physical coupling is particularly beneficial for variables governed by more complex exchange processes. Moreover, the learned parameters closely match the true values for the dominant coefficients, confirming that the method captures meaningful physical structure rather than merely fitting the data. While the present study is limited to a controlled synthetic setting, the results suggest that physics-informed learning provides a robust and interpretable framework for greenhouse climate modeling in data-limited regimes. Future work will focus on validation using real greenhouse measurements and on extending the approach to more detailed and higher-dimensional models. These findings support the use of physics-informed learning as a practical tool for reliable greenhouse climate monitoring in data-limited environments.
