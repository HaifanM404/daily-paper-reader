# An online adaptive finite-element method for nonsmooth PDE-constrained optimization ∗

Harbir Antil † Robert J. Baraldi ‡ Rohit Khandelwal § Drew P. Kouri ‡

April 28, 2026 

Abstract 

We present a trust-region-based adaptive finite-element algorithm for numerically solving a class of nonsmooth PDE-constrained optimization problems that includes problems with spar-sifying regularizers and convex constraints. In particular, we consider the class of problems whose objective function is the sum of a smooth, possibly nonconvex, function and a nons-mooth extended real-valued convex function. Our method combines the robustness of inexact trust-region algorithms for nonsmooth problems with the efficiency of adaptive finite-element discretizations. Starting from a coarse mesh, the algorithm automatically refines the discretiza-tion based on reliable a posteriori error estimators for both the state and adjoint equations, systematically controlling the accuracy of the computed smooth objective function value and gradient. This adaptivity mechanism balances computational cost and solution accuracy, en-abling high resolution of localized phenomena and sparsity structures in the state and control variables. We demonstrate the performance of our algorithm through numerical experiments on representative control and topology optimization examples.

# 1 Introduction 

We consider the optimization problem min

ϕ : Z → (−∞ , +∞], and u = S(z) ∈ U is the solution to a system of partial differential equations (PDEs), i.e., c(u, z ) = 0 ∈ V ∗. Here, V is a reflexive Banach space, V∗ its dual space, and 

c : U × Z → V ∗ represents a weak form of the PDE, which we assume to be sufficiently regular as to admit derivatives of the solution map z 7 → S(z). Commonly V = U [36], but we do not make this assumption. Algorithms for solving optimization problems constrained by PDEs (1) must balance conver-gence robustness with the computational cost of solving the PDE. In applications where solutions may exhibit sharp interfaces (e.g., topology optimization [31]), memory limitations [3, 10, 44], or high-dimensional uncertainties [30, 38, 39, 54, 58], solvers must be able to handle inexactness arising from coarse discretizations or early termination of iterative linear/nonlinear PDE solvers. Many optimization algorithms can leverage various forms of inexactness, such as trust-region [10, 37, 54], linesearch [25, 48], and sequential quadratic programming (SQP) methods [32, 33, 50, 55, 56]. Modern inexact trust-region algorithms build upon the early developments of Mor´ e [43] and Carter [20, 21], and control inexactness automatically during optimization. Examples include: inexact linear solves [6], adaptive quadrature [38, 39], reduced-order models [28, 54, 58], and randomized compression [10, 44]. Similarly, SQP methods can be modified to incorporate inexactness in both the solution of the quadratic subproblem and objective values and gradients [18, 32, 33, 56]. In particular, [50, 55, 56] bear the closest resemblance to the present work in their algorithmic con-trol of adaptive finite elements; however, these methods do not handle nonsmoothness aside from convex control constraints [55]. Similar analysis was employed by [14] for nonlinear least-squares methods with inexact objective values and gradients under similar assumptions as [6]. Inexactness in step computation for nonsmooth methods has been studied in [25, 42], but the objective value and gradient are exact. We build upon recent advances in nonsmooth trust-region algorithms [6] for infinite-dimensional optimization problems (1). Specifically, [6, Algorithm 1] demonstrates rapid convergence [7], subproblem solver flexibility [8], and inexactness [6, 10]. With respect to the latter, [6, Algorithm 1] converges even when the objective value and gradient are evaluated up to some algorithmically determined tolerance. We utilize this algorithm to provide a robust foundation for challenging PDE-constrained optimization problems via adaptive finite-element discretizations using residual-based error indicators of the state and adjoint equations. Adaptive finite-element methods (AFEM) [1, 5, 16, 51] provide an effective mechanism for reducing computational cost while maintaining sufficient accuracy required for solving the PDE. AFEM typically exploits three types of adaptivity (or combinations thereof): ( i) h-adaptivity that refines the mesh [23, 46]; ( ii ) p-adaptivity that increases the polynomial order of the elements [23]; and ( iii ) r-adaptivity that relocates mesh nodes [17, 19]. Guiding the aforementioned types of refinement are various estimators that quantify the discretization error associated with the ap-proximation of the infinite-dimensional space. The estimator and adaptivity combination is often tailored to the optimization problem; c.f. [13, 15, 35, 53]. For instance, goal-oriented AFEM aims to perform adaptivity by minimizing an error most relevant to a desired quantity of interest rather than a global norm, e.g., energy norms in the case of Navier-Stokes simulation [15] and variational inequalities [35]. A more mechanical approach involves solving a full optimization problem, refining, 2then repeating the process until a particular refinement level or error is attained [11, 12, 52, 53]; this technique of optimize-then-refine often lacks rigorous convergence guarantees. In concert with these techniques, a variety of a posteriori error estimators exist that indicate which cells should be refined [5]. A common type is the averaging ZZ-estimator [29, 57], which was derived for higher-order elements in [50, 56]. Along with ZZ estimators, residual-based error estimators can be used for online refinement. For instance, the aforementioned Ulbrich and Ziems work [56] proposed an inexact adaptive SQP method with residual-driven refinement for nonlinear PDE-constrained op-timization. This SQP algorithm was later generalized for time-dependent PDEs in [50] and control constraints in [55]. More recently, [2] develop AFEM for sparse PDE-constrained optimization and [47] performed adaptivity for regularized problems (1). In our work, we use reliable , residual-based estimators that involve the state or adjoint equations. Reliable estimators provide upper bounds on the true, infinite-dimensional error and hence indicate where the mesh should be refined to improve the approximation quality [5, 19]. Our paper is organized in the following way. In Section 2, we discuss the problem formulation and illustrate the general concepts behind our finite-element implementation in Section 3. In Sec-tion 4, we introduce the nonsmooth trust-region algorithm and discuss how it handles inexactness. We conclude in Section 5, demonstrating the performance of our approach on numerical examples.
