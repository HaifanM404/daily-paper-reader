# A Neural Network Method for Scalar Conservation Laws with Convergence Rates for Shock-Wave Solutions

Abstract 

We introduce an entropy-compatible neural network method for scalar hyperbolic conservation laws based on a computable surrogate of the Kružkov entropy residual. The proposed loss is designed to enforce weak consistency and entropy admissibility while remaining amenable to rigorous analysis. For piecewise smooth entropy solutions, we construct explicit neural network competitors with provably small loss by combining shock-adapted continuous piecewise linear approximations with representability results for neural networks. These approximation bounds, together with entropy-based stability estimates, yield rigorous L1 error estimates for minimizers of the resulting loss functional. In particular, when the network size scales comparably to the number of degrees of freedom of a space time mesh of size h, we recover the classical O(h1/2) convergence rate in shock-dominated regimes. The analysis also covers solutions containing rarefaction waves and regular shock interactions; in this extended version, we further treat smooth initial data that develop a shock in finite time, for which an L1 estimate of order O(h1/2| ln h|) is obtained. Numerical experiments in one and two space dimensions illustrate the effectiveness of the method and its ability to resolve nonsmooth wave structures accurately. 

Keywords: Hyperbolic conservation laws, entropy solutions, neural networks, PINNs, entropy stability, error estimates.

Over the past several decades, extensive research has focused on developing numerical schemes for hyperbolic conservation laws. Early foundational methods, such as the Lax-Friedrichs and Godunov schemes, offer robust shock-capturing but are limited to first-order accuracy due to numerical diffusion and Godunov’s theorem for linear monotone schemes. Higher-order approaches improve accu-racy in smooth regions but may introduce nonphysical oscillations near discontinuities. To mitigate such oscillations while retaining higher accuracy, total variation diminishing (TVD) and monotone upstream-centered schemes for conservation laws (MUSCL) employ limiters, and further advances led to nonlinear high-resolution methods such as ENO [1], WENO [2, 3], RKDG [4] and entropy-stable method [5]. On the theoretical side, the convergence analysis of numerical schemes can be traced back to Kružkov’s seminal work [6], which established the notion of entropy solutions ensuring uniqueness and stability. Building upon this, Kuznetsov [7] derived the first a priori L∞(L1) error estimate of order O(h1/2) for monotone schemes on uniform grids, with Sanders [8] extending the result to non-uniform Cartesian meshes. Subsequent contributions, such as those by Cockburn and Gremaud [9] and Makridakis and Perthame [10], proposed alternative techniques achieving the same convergence order. Besides, Nessyahu and Tadmor [11] proved that several finite volume schemes, converge at rate O(h) in the Lip ′ norm (the Wasserstein distance), for Lip +-bounded initial data. Another way to prove conver-gence to the entropy solution is DiPerna’s theory of measure-valued function convergence [12], which was first applied by Szepessy in 1989 [13] to the streamline diffusion method and was later applied to finite difference, finite volume, and various space time DG methods. Alongside these classical developments, neural network methods have recently emerged as an alter-native computational approach for PDEs. Representative methods include physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) and their variants [14, 15], the neural network Ritz method [16, 17], operator learn-ing approaches including Fourier neural operator (FNO) [18] and DeepONet [19], and dynamically parametrized neural network methods for time evolving problems [20–22]. Among these, PINNs are particularly natural for solving PDEs by directly penalizing the PDE residual, and their theoretical properties have recently been studied in [23, 24]. Let UNN denote a neural network approximation. For hyperbolic conservation laws, since the standard PINN formulations penalize the strong form equation residual ∂tUNN + ∇x · f (UNN ) in L2 norm, one cannot, in general, expect the PINN loss to remain small for discontinuous solutions; see [25]. This limitation motivates the design of alternative loss functionals [26]. A common remedy is to introduce artificial viscosity. Another alternative is to enforce the PDE in weak form or in finite volume form [27], which are compatible with discontinuous solutions. Since weak solutions are generally nonunique, one must additionally impose an entropy condition to select the physically relevant solution. This has motivated incorporating entropy constraints into the loss [28]. Related rigorous approximation and generalization results for neural network approximations of parametric hyperbolic conservation laws were established in [29]. Moreover, under the assumption that the loss can be made arbitrarily small, [30] derived an error estimate for their neural-network method by invoking the Kružkov-type estimates of Bouchut and Perthame [31]. For scalar HCLs, the Kružkov entropy family becomes particularly important. Specifically, the associated Kružkov entropy residual plays two roles simultaneously. First, it recovers the conservation 2law in the weak sense. Indeed, by taking k ∈ R large or small enough, the full family of Kružkov entropy inequalities (3) recovers the weak formulation (2). Second, for general k ∈ R, (3) enforces entropy admissibility and thereby selects the physically relevant solution, restoring uniqueness and the classical 

L1-stability property. Thus, the Kružkov entropy residual simultaneously measures weak consistency 

and entropy admissibility . This observation is the common starting point of both weak PINNs and the present work. The weak PINN (wPINN) [32] leverages these dual effects by enforcing the entropy inequality in a distributional sense. A central ingredient of our analysis is a constructive bridge between finite element approxima-tions and neural networks. We first construct continuous piecewise linear (CPwL) competitors on shock-adapted simplicial meshes producing provably small loss, and we then use representability and approximation results for CPwL functions by neural networks to transfer these constructions to the neural setting. This yields explicit neural network competitors and, consequently, an a priori upper bound on the loss attained by a minimizer of the training problem. Combined with entropy-based stability estimates, this leads to rigorous convergence rates for piecewise smooth entropy solutions 3containing shocks. In particular, when the network size scales comparably to the number of degrees of freedom of a space time mesh of size h, namely O(h−(d+1) ) nonzero parameters up to dimension-dependent constants; see Remark 3, the resulting neural approximation converges in L1 at rate O(h1/2)

in shock-dominated regimes, matching the classical benchmark for monotone schemes. We emphasize, however, that this half-order rate should be interpreted as a consequence of the current proof tech-nique rather than as an intrinsic limitation of the method. Indeed, the explicit neural competitors constructed in Lemma 2 already satisfy a first-order estimate, while the numerical experiments in Sub-section 5.1.4 and Remark 5 exhibit nearly first-order convergence. Taken together, these observations suggest that the practical accuracy of the proposed method may exceed the rate presently guaranteed by the rigorous analysis. Our contributions are summarized as follows. For scalar HCLs, we introduce a Kružkov entropy residual loss functional and prove the existence of neural networks that make this loss functional small. Combining these ingredients with entropy stability arguments, we derive rigorous L1 convergence rates for piecewise smooth entropy solutions containing shocks and rarefaction waves. Section 2 introduces the problem setting and the proposed loss functional. Sections 3-4 develop convergence results for one- and multidimensional settings under piecewise smoothness assump-tions. Section 5 presents numerical experiments. Some proofs and auxiliary approximation results are collected in the appendix.

3.1 Single shock waves and shock interactions 

This section establishes the a priori error estimate for the single-shock case. The proof has two main ingredients. First, we construct a neural network competitor with O(h−(d+1) ) neurons whose loss is sufficiently small, which is highly nontrivial. Indeed, we will convert derivatives in the loss into jumps across the shock, which has favorable signs for estimation due to the entropy condition (8). ## 4 Error estimate for d-dimensional scalar hyperbolic conservation laws

We therefore focus on the genuinely new situation in several space dimensions: the interaction of several shock hypersurfaces.
