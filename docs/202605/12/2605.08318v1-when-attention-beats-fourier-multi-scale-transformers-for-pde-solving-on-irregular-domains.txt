# When Attention Beats Fourier: Multi-Scale Transformers for PDE Solving on Irregular Domains

# Abstract 

We study the problem of architecture selection for deep learning models trained to solve partial differential equations (PDEs), asking when transformer-based architectures with learned attention outperform Fourier-domain neural operators. We introduce the Multi-Scale Attention Transformer (MSAT), a deep learning architecture that encodes spatiotemporal solution histories as token sequences and trains end-to-end via a composite supervised objective with optional physics-informed regularization terms. We conduct a comprehensive empirical evaluation against nine baselines—including physics-informed neural networks (PINNs), neural operators (FNO, DeepONet, GNOT), and state-space models (Mamba-NO)—across five benchmark problems from the PINNacle suite, using identical train/test splits and reference data for all methods. MSAT achieves state-of-the-art generalization on complex geometry problems ( L2rel = 0 .0101 on Heat2D-CG, a 3.7× improvement over FNO) at 34 s total inference vs. 120 ,812 s for Mamba-NO. Ablation studies over the physics regularization component reveal a precise inductive bias tradeoff: physics priors reduce test error on diffusion-dominated problems but degrade generalization on chaotic and recirculating-flow regimes, directly characterizing the prior misspecification boundary. Approximation error bounds as a function of domain boundary complexity κ provide a theoretical basis for these empirical findings and a principled rule for architecture selection. 

# 1 Introduction 

Deep learning has emerged as a powerful paradigm for scientific computing, with neural network architectures trained end-to-end to approximate the solution maps of partial differential equations (PDEs). The central open question in this area is not whether deep learning can solve PDEs—it clearly can—but which architecture and training objective is most appropriate for a given problem class .This paper answers that question empirically and theoretically for the class of complex geometry problems. Two architectural families dominate the literature. Physics-Informed Neural Networks 

(PINNs) Raissi et al. [2019] train a network to minimize a composite loss of supervised data fit and PDE residuals at collocation points, exploiting automatic differentiation to enforce governing equations as a regularizer. PINNs generalize well on steady-state problems with well-posed residuals but exhibit poor generalization on chaotic systems, high-frequency solutions, and long rollouts Wang et al. [2021], Krishnapriyan et al. [2021]. 

Neural operators Kovachki et al. [2023] take a function-space learning perspective: they train deep networks to map between spaces of input functions and output functions, enabling zero-shot generalization to new initial conditions. The Fourier Neural Operator (FNO) Li et al. [2021] parametrizes the kernel of the solution operator in frequency space via learned spectral convolutions,

achieving strong generalization on smooth periodic benchmarks. However, FNO’s architecture encodes a hard inductive bias: spectral truncation to the lowest K Fourier modes. On domains with irregular geometry, this truncation systematically discards high-frequency modes excited by boundary effects, limiting generalization. Transformer architectures Vaswani et al. [2017] compute data-dependent, position-wise attention over all input tokens, implementing a learned kernel that adapts to the input distribution without fixed-basis constraints. This makes transformers natural candidates for irregular geometry settings where FNO’s spectral inductive bias is mismatched. We test this hypothesis systematically through a large-scale empirical evaluation, supported by approximation-theoretic analysis.

# 2 Related Work 

Physics-Informed Neural Networks. Raissi et al. Raissi et al. [2019] introduced PINNs as a supervised learning framework that augments the training objective with PDE residual terms evaluated at collocation points via automatic differentiation. Subsequent work addressed training instabilities— including gradient imbalance between loss terms and spectral bias in deep networks—through adaptive loss weighting Wang et al. [2024] (PINN-LRA), residual-adaptive resampling Lu et al. [2021b] (RAR), and domain decomposition Moseley et al. [2023] (FBPINN). Despite these advances, PINNs exhibit poor generalization on chaotic and high-frequency regimes Krishnapriyan et al. [2021], a phenomenon attributed to the mismatch between the collocation prior and the problem’s frequency content. 

Neural Operators and Deep Learning Architectures for PDEs. Neural operators frame PDE solving as a function-space learning problem, training deep networks to generalize across initial conditions. DeepONet Lu et al. [2021a] uses a branch/trunk decomposition inspired by the universal approximation theorem for operators. FNO Li et al. [2021] parametrizes the solution operator in frequency space via learnable spectral convolutions, achieving strong generalization on smooth benchmarks with periodic structure. GNOT Hao et al. [2023] replaces spectral convolutions with cross-attention, extending operator learning to heterogeneous geometries. Mamba-NO applies selective state-space models Gu and Dao [2024] as neural operators, achieving competitive accuracy on structured benchmarks but at impractical cost in the sequential-scan formulation. 

Transformers and Attention Mechanisms in Machine Learning. The transformer architec-ture Vaswani et al. [2017] has become the dominant deep learning paradigm across modalities, with scaled dot-product attention enabling data-dependent, position-wise representation learning that generalizes well across diverse input distributions. Recent work has applied attention to scientific computing Li et al. [2024], Cao [2021], demonstrating that learned kernels can outperform fixed-basis operators on non-periodic domains. Our work provides the first systematic benchmark comparing transformers, spectral operators, SSMs, and PINNs on a diverse PDE suite, with ablations isolating the contribution of physics-informed regularization as a training signal. 

Inductive Bias and Architecture Selection in Deep Learning. The question of which neural network architecture best matches a given problem structure is a central theme in machine learning 2theory ?. Spectral methods encode a periodic inductive bias; convolutional networks encode transla-tion equivariance; attention encodes permutation equivariance with global receptive field. Our work characterizes this question empirically and theoretically for the PDE domain, establishing boundary complexity κ as the key variable governing architecture selection.

Remark 3.1. The original architecture description used scale factors {1, 7, 30 } motivated by daily/weekly/monthly temporal patterns in hydrology applications. For the PDE benchmark, we use powers-of-two scales {1, 2, 4, 8}, which are better suited to the uniform temporal grids of the PINNacle datasets and avoid introducing domain-specific inductive bias into the architecture comparison. 

3.3 Physics-Informed Constraints 

When physics constraints are enabled, MSAT appends a PhysicsInformedLayer that maps the latent representation z to constraint residuals via sub-networks for PDE-specific physical priors. For the PDE benchmark, we implement three generic constraint types: (i) a mass conservation residual 

Lmass that penalizes divergence of the predicted flux field, (ii) an energy dissipation residual Lenergy 

that penalizes non-monotone energy growth in dissipative systems, and (iii) a spatial smoothness 

regularizer Lsmooth that penalizes large second-order spatial gradients: 

Lphys = λ1Lmass + λ2Lenergy + λ3Lsmooth ,

where λi ≥ 0 are learnable non-negative scalar weights initialized to 0.1. The total training objective is: 

L = LMSE + Lphys ,

3where LMSE = ∥ˆu − u∥22/∥u∥22 is the normalized mean-squared error on labeled data. In our PDE benchmark, we treat the physics layer as an ablation variable to characterize its domain dependence (Section 4.4). 

Remark 3.2. The physics constraint terms encode smoothness assumptions that are well-matched to diffusion-dominated and advection-diffusion problems but are misspecified for chaotic systems (Kuramoto-Sivashinsky) and unsteady recirculating flows (NS2D lid-driven cavity). This misspecifi-cation is the primary driver of the performance degradation observed in the ablation study. 

3.4 Implementation Details 

MSAT uses embedding dimension d = 256 , H = 8 attention heads, L = 6 transformer layers, FFN hidden dimension 512, and S = 4 attention scales. Training uses Adam with initial learning rate 

η = 10 −3, weight decay 10 −4, ReduceLROnPlateau scheduler (factor 0.5, patience 5), and early stopping (patience 20). All experiments use PINNacle COMSOL reference solutions as ground truth, loaded via the same data pipeline as FNO/GNOT/DeepONet baselines to ensure fair comparison. Batch size 64, bf16 AMP on A100. The Pareto plot (omitted for space) plots L2rel on Heat2D-CG against total compute time. MSAT (no physics) achieves the best accuracy at 34 seconds total inference. FNO achieves 3.7× worse accuracy at comparable cost (634 s). Mamba-NO achieves 2.1× worse accuracy at 3,553 × higher cost. The Pareto frontier on geometry-rich problems is clearly occupied by MSAT. We note that Mamba-NO’s runtime reflects the pure-PyTorch sequential scan implementation, which scales as O(N ) per layer for sequence length N without the compiled CUDA kernels required for the fast parallel scan. # 5 Discussion and Limitations 

Limitations. MSAT is a point-prediction model: it predicts u at individual spatiotemporal points rather than learning a full solution operator. This means it requires a new forward pass per query point, unlike FNO which predicts the full field in one pass. For applications requiring dense field prediction, neural operators may be preferable despite their accuracy disadvantage on complex geometry. The physics constraints are generic (mass conservation, energy dissipation, spatial smoothness) and are not derived from the specific governing equations of each benchmark PDE. Future work should investigate automatic constraint derivation from symbolic PDE specifications, enabling problem-agnostic physics-informed training. MSAT’s strong Heat2D-CG performance comes at modestly higher training complexity than FNO; the early stopping mechanism mitigates this in practice but hyperparameter sensitivity warrants further study. 

Broader implications. Our results suggest that the field’s current emphasis on spectral neural oper-ators may underserve applications in complex geometry domains, which are common in engineering practice (multi-material composites, fracture mechanics, biological tissue modeling). Attention-based architectures deserve more systematic investigation in these regimes. 

# 6 Conclusion 

We have presented MSAT, a multi-scale attention architecture for PDE solving, and evaluated it comprehensively against the major families of physics-informed ML methods. The central finding is that MSAT achieves state-of-the-art accuracy on complex geometry diffusion problems ( 3.7×

improvement over FNO on Heat2D-CG) at negligible inference cost, while competitive methods that achieve comparable accuracy on other benchmarks (Mamba-NO on KS) do so at three orders of magnitude higher compute cost. Ablation studies identify the precise regime where physics-informed priors help: diffusion-dominated problems where the prior’s smoothness assumptions are satisfied. These findings motivate a theory of architecture-problem alignment for neural PDE solvers: spectral methods with periodic inductive bias excel on smooth periodic problems; attention-based methods excel on irregular geometry; collocation-based PINNs excel on steady-state problems with well-posed residuals.
