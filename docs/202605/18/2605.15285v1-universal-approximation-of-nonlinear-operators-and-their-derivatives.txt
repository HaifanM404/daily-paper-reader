# Universal Approximation of Nonlinear Operators and Their Derivatives 

## Filippo de Feo ‚ąó

## May 18, 2026 

Abstract 

Derivative-Informed Operator Learning (DIOL), i.e. learning a (nonlinear) operator and its derivatives, is an open research frontier at the foundations of the influential field of Operator Learning (OL). In particular, Universal Approximation Theorems (UATs) of nonlinear operators and their derivatives are foundational open questions and delicate problems in nonlinear functional analysis. In this manuscript, we prove the first UATs of non-linear 

k-times differentiable operators between Banach spaces and their derivatives, uniformly on compact sets and in weighted Sobolev norms for general finite input measures, via OL architectures. Our results are the first complete generalizations of the corresponding influential classical results in [Hornik, 1991] to infinite-dimensional settings and OL. We discuss several open areas where DIOL and our UATs find applications: high-order accuracy in OL, fast constrained optimization in Banach spaces (e.g. optimal control of PDEs, inverse problems) and numerical methods for infinite-dimensional PDEs (e.g. HJB PDEs on Banach spaces from optimal control of PDEs, SPDEs, path-dependent systems, partially observed systems, mean-field control). We parameterize nonlinear operators via Encoder-Decoder Architectures (EDAs), renowned classes of OL architectures due to their generality, including classical architectures, such as DeepONets, Deep-H-ONets, PCA-Nets. Our results are based on four key features that allow us to prove UATs in full generality: (i) Approximation Properties of Banach spaces. (ii) k-times continuous differentiability in the sense of Bastiani (weaker than k-times continuous Fr√©chet differentiability). (iii) Natural compact-open topologies for UA; indeed, we show that UA in standard compact-open topologies induced by operator norms is violated even for Fr√©chet derivatives. (iv) Construction of novel weighted Sobolev spaces for the UA. 

Keywords : Operator Learning, Derivative-Informed Operator Learning, Sobolev spaces on Banach spaces, Learn-then-Optimize, Optimize-then-Learn, Constrained Optimization on Banach spaces, Infinite-dimensional PDEs, Infinite-Dimensional Optimal Control

Operator Learning. Over roughly the last seven years, operator learning has emerged as a central paradigm for learning maps between infinite-dimensional function spaces. OL has been extensively used to learn maps from coefficient fields, forcing terms, initial or boundary data, and other functional inputs to the corresponding par-tial differential equation (PDE) solutions or observables, providing fast surrogate models for many-query scientific computing. Beyond classical PDE solution surrogates, OL has been used for scientific discovery and data-driven modeling, nonlinear reduced-order modeling, Koopman/operator-inference methods, solver acceleration, data-driven closure and constitutive modeling, turbulence and continuum-model learning, computer-graphics shape and surface maps, and weather-style forecasting operators. Standard architectures include the Encoder-Decoder Architectures [75], DeepONet [23, 83], Fourier Neural Operator [74], PCA-Net [8], Deep-H-ONet/Hilbert‚ÄďGalerkin Neural Op-erator [20] (see also [25]), Laplace Neural Operator [17], Spectral Neural Operator [41], and Convolutional Neural Operator [109]. Their use for these tasks is theoretically supported by a series of influential UATs for nonlinear operators on infinite-dimensional spaces [23, 74, 8, 64, 83, 20, 21, 65, 73, 67, 77, 131]. We refer to [75] for a detailed review of OL. We also refer to [31, 22, 56, 86] for signature learning and UATs there, to [78, 102, 90] for OL on Wasserstein spaces. 

Derivative-Informed Operator Learning. Even more recently, DIOL has been rapidly growing [15, 16, 25, 52, 53, 84, 55, 98, 97, 107, 129]. Due to the theoretical and computational challenges, numerical results are mostly restricted to the separable Hilbert setting and to the first derivative case 1. Here, similarly to the finite dimensional case, the goal is to learn an operator and its (infinite-dimensional) derivatives. This offers, at least, three practical applications (we refer to Section 2 for extensive examples): (i) higher-order accuracy than standard OL with a significantly lower training sample size and generation cost [97, 16, 107, 15, 85, 52]. (ii) Applications of OL to constrained optimization in infinite-dimensional spaces (such as optimal control of PDEs, as inverse problems, and optimal design) [129, 85, 52, 53], where derivatives encode fundamental information on minimizers, thereby allowing for fast online optimization. (iii) Development of numerical methods for infinite-dimensional PDEs [25], such as functional differential equations in Physics and Kolmogorov and Hamilton‚ÄďJacobi‚ÄďBellman PDEs from infinite-dimensional optimal control and games (controlled PDEs, SPDEs, path-dependent systems, mean-field control, and partially observed stochastic systems), where the (infinite-dimensional) derivatives of the solution appear naturally in the infinite-dimensional PDE. However, the literature on Derivative-Informed Operator learning is mainly empirical and, unfortunately, UATs for the simultaneous approximation of nonlinear operators and their infinite-dimensional derivatives are not under-stood. Indeed, to the best of our knowledge, the only available results are proven in the following very special cases (all appeared within about one year with respect to the first appearance of the present manuscript on arXiv):

## 1.3 Our Contributions 

It is evident from the literature review above that Derivative-Informed Operator Learning is an open DIOL research frontier at the foundations of OL, spanning theoretical, computational, and application-driven challenges, with possible implications in all applied sciences. In particular, UATs of nonlinear operators and their derivatives are foundational open questions. Therefore, the goal of the present manuscript is to provide the influential field of OL with the theoretical foundations for the simultaneous learning of operators and their derivatives. We summarize the structure of the paper and our contributions as follows. 

Motivations for Derivative-Informed Operator Learning (Section 2). As promised at the beginning of the introduction, we start by presenting several motivations showing the broad relevance of DIOL and, consequently, of UATs of nonlinear operators and their derivatives. While, in this section, we mostly avoid mathematical rigor, we think that the presentation is to be considered an equally important contribution of the paper, as we launch several new challenges and applications of DIOL. Motivations for DIOL typically come from query tasks (see Definition 2.1), requiring fast evaluation of a target k-times differentiable operator F : X ‚Üí Y and, possibly, its derivatives. This leads to higher-order accuracy in OL, as well as to Learn-then-Optimize methods for fast online constrained optimization in Banach spaces (such as optimal control of PDEs, inverse problems, or optimal design) and to nu-merical methods (conjectured as Deep Banach‚ÄďGalerkin Methods) for infinite-dimensional PDEs on Banach spaces, such as functional differential equations in physics (e.g. the celebrated Hopf equation in turbulence theory and Schwinger‚ÄďDyson equation in quantum field theory) and HJB PDEs from infinite-dimensional optimal control (in-cluding problems of PDEs, SPDEs, path-dependent systems, partially observed stochastic systems, and mean-field SDEs). We refer to Section 2 for extensive bibliographic references.

In Section 3, we introduce Encoder-Decoder Architectures (i.e. (1); see Definition 3.4), renowned classes of architectures in OL due to their applicability these general contexts. In particular, in [25] F was parameterized via an HGNO/Deep-H-ONet F EXd ,őł, 1 ‚ąą HGN O (X, R) (see Subsection 3.3). UATs proved there, up to second order Fr√©chet derivatives and for unbounded operators applied to the first derivative, ensured that HGNOs were able to approximately solve the PDE (under suitable novel continuity 11 assumptions on the fully nonlinear operator G in the variable D2F ), by minimizing the L2((0 , T ) √ó X; dt ‚äó őľ)-norm of the residual (9) of the PDE (8) over the Hilbert space X. This is the setting of Derivative-Informed Operator Learning because it relies on accurately representing F, DF, D 2F simultaneously via F EXd ,őł, 1, DF EXd ,őł, 1, D 2F EXd ,őł, 1,and in particular, in representing D2F , the Fr√©chet derivative of the non-linear operator DF , appearing in the PDE (8). In HJB equations from optimal control (see Section 2.3.2), feedback control operators u : X ‚Üí Z (where Z

is Hilbert space) are also parameterized by HGNO U EXd ,őł, DZm ‚ąą HGN O (X, Z ), which is trained, i.e. informed, by the derivatives DF EXd ,őł, 1, D 2F EXd ,őł, 1. In this case, UATs proved in [25] allowed to achieve UA of optimal feedback controls via DF EXd ,őł, 1, D 2F EXd ,őł, 1. Numerical tests on optimal control problems of deterministic and stochastic PDEs validated the analytical results. 

Banach space case. Let now X be a separable Banach space with the Bounded Approximation Property (As-sumption A.20). Inspired by the above methods, we conjecture methods that minimize (9) over Encoder-Decoder Architectures (Definition 3.4): it may be expected that suitable UATs (such as the ones proved in the present manuscript or suitable adaptations 10 ) for nonlinear operators and their derivatives lead to Deep Banach‚ÄďGalerkin Methods , i.e. generalizations of the previous numerical methods to the case where X is a Banach space, serving us as an important motivations to derive these UATs in the present manuscript. However, the development of Deep Banach‚ÄďGalerkin Methods is expected to be even more challenging than [25], where the Hilbert structure was heavily exploited, and it is left for future investigations. Next, to illustrate the potential of PDEs of the form (8), and thus motivate numerical methods and UATs for their solution, we present several examples.
