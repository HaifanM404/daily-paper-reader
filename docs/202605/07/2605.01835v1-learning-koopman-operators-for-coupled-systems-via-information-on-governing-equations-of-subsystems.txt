## Learning Koopman operators for coupled systems via information on governing equations of subsystems 

Tatsuya Naoi and Jun Ohkubo 

Graduate School of Science and Engineering, Saitama University, Sakura, Saitama, 338-8570, Japan 

Nonlinear coupled systems are ubiquitous in science and engineering. The analysis and modeling of such systems is challenging due to their high dimensionality and complex interactions among subsystems. In recent years, operator-theoretic methods based on the Koopman operator have attracted attention as a powerful tool for analyzing and modeling nonlinear dynamical systems. Extended dynamic mode decomposition (EDMD) is one of the most popular methods to approximate the Koopman operator. However, EDMD is a purely data-driven method, and it could be unstable and inaccurate for coupled systems under limited data availability. In this paper, we propose a method to learn the Koopman operator for coupled systems using the differential equations governing each subsystem. We also demonstrate its effectiveness through numerical experiments on coupled oscillator systems. 

I. INTRODUCTION 

Coupled nonlinear dynamical systems are prevalent in various fields, including physics, engineering, biology, and social sciences. Examples of coupled systems include cou-pled oscillators, power grids, and multi-agent networks. Analyzing and modeling such systems is challenging due to their high dimensionality and complex interactions among subsystems. Coupled systems have been extensively studied in physics. One example is synchronization phenomena [1– 4]. While synchronization provides an important per-spective on the collective behavior of coupled systems, it does not by itself address how to construct compact and predictive representations of coupled nonlinear dy-namical systems. As a result, a variety of data-driven methods have been developed to learn predictive models of coupled nonlinear dynamical systems [5–7]. System identification methods have been proposed to infer the governing equations from data [8, 9]. Among these data-driven methods, operator-theoretic methods based on the Koopman operator [10] have at-tracted increasing attention. They provide a linear rep-resentation of nonlinear dynamics in an approximately infinite-dimensional function space [11]. For example, extended dynamic mode decomposition (EDMD) is a data-driven method to learn the Koopman operator [12]. Although the Koopman operator is generally infinite-dimensional, its spectral properties enable modal de-composition and stability analysis of nonlinear dynam-ical systems in terms of eigenvalues, eigenfunctions, and modes [13–16]. The linear representation of nonlinear dynamics also enables the application of various linear control techniques [17, 18]. The application of Koopman operator theory to cou-pled systems has also been studied [19–21]. These stud-ies have proposed more effective approaches to learn the Koopman operator for coupled systems by leveraging their structure and interactions. In addition, Koopman-based analysis has led to an investigation of the phase-space structure of coupled oscillator systems [22] and the synchronization of oscillators [23]. However, EDMD-based approaches can face significant challenges when ap-plied to nonlinear systems with multiple time scales and high dimensionality [24]. The incorporation of prior knowledge and physical con-straints into the learning process could improve the ac-curacy and physical consistency of Koopman operator approximations. For example, system structure, such as interaction topology, has been embedded into the learn-ing process of Koopman operator approximation [20, 21]. Additionally, some works have incorporated conservation laws and other physical properties into the learning pro-cess of EDMD [25–27]. However, most existing methods are purely data-driven approaches. In many real-world applications, while the governing equations of the entire system may be unknown, the differential equations governing each sub-system or parts of the system are often known as prior knowledge. In such cases, it is advantageous to leverage this prior knowledge of governing differential equations for each subsystem when learning the Koopman opera-tor of the entire system. Some studies have utilized the governing differential equations to derive the Koopman operator [28, 29]. However, to the best of our knowledge, there is no study on coupled systems that utilizes par-tial prior knowledge, such as the governing differential equations of each subsystem. In this paper, we propose a method to learn the Koop-man operator for coupled systems by utilizing the dif-ferential equations governing each subsystem. The pro-posed method consists of two main steps. First, we derive the Koopman matrices for each subsystem from the dif-ferential equations governing the subsystems using the method proposed in Ref. [30, 31]. Next, we construct the global Koopman matrix by combining the matrices for each subsystem using the online EDMD method [29, 32]. We demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method through numerical experiments on coupled oscillator sys-tems. The rest of this paper is organized as follows. In Sec. II, we review the coupled systems and the Koopman opera-

EDMD. In the proposed method, we first derive local Koopman matrices for each subsystem from differential equations governing the subsystems. Then, we construct a global Koopman matrix by combining the local Koop-man matrices. Finally, we update the global Koopman matrix using data with the aid of online EDMD. Nu-merical experiments on coupled Duffing oscillators and coupled van der Pol oscillators demonstrated that the proposed method predicts the time evolution of the state vectors more accurately than EDMD with less data. These results indicate that the proposed method enables effective learning of the Koopman matrix of coupled sys-tems. By combining existing Koopman-based methods for coupled systems [22, 23] with the proposed method, we may gain further insight into the dynamics of coupled systems. When the coupling strength between subsystems is moderate, the differential equations of the individual sub-systems primarily govern the behavior of the entire sys-tem. By contrast, when the coupling is strong, the behav-ior of the entire system is governed not by the dynamics of the individual subsystems but by the interactions be-tween subsystems. In such cases, deriving local Koopman matrices from the differential equations of the individual subsystems may not provide a good initial approximation to the global Koopman matrix. Therefore, it is necessary to investigate how the coupling strength affects the per-formance of the proposed method in future work. Furthermore, in this paper, we assumed that the pa-rameters in the differential equations for the subsystems are known a priori. However, in many practical situa-tions, the form of the equations may be known while the parameters remain unknown. Reference [29] discusses the case of unknown equation parameters, in which the initial Koopman matrix is derived from equations with 9guessed parameter values and then updated using data with the aid of online EDMD. Therefore, we can also con-sider the case in which the parameters in the equations are unknown for coupled systems. There is another important challenge for future work. Our proposed method suffers from the curse of dimen-sionality when the number of subsystems becomes large. In general, the number of dictionary functions increases exponentially as the number of subsystems increases. Therefore, it is necessary to consider an effective way to handle a larger number of subsystems. One possible approach is to employ the tensor-train format (decompo-sition) [34]. Some studies have applied the tensor-train format to EDMD [35, 36]. However, no existing method combines online EDMD with the tensor-train format. Therefore, it is necessary to develop an online EDMD method using the tensor-train format. Another possi-ble approach is to reduce the number of dictionary func-tions. In Ref. [37], Li et al. proposed a method for learn-ing dictionary functions using a neural network. These directions would constitute important steps toward scal-able and data-efficient learning of Koopman operators for large-scale coupled systems.
